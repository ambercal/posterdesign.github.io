1880-1895: the birth of Lithographic poster

The evolution and development of poster art has always been closely
linked to technical advances in printmaking, notably lithography.
Lithography was invented by Alois Senefelder (1771-1834) as far back
as 1798, but for decades it was too slow and expensive for poster
production. Most posterscontinued to be simple wood or metal engravings
with little colour or design - they were used to convey only the most
important information from the government, as well as occasional advertising
with hierarchal typography. The 19th century witnessed the advent of
chromolithography though, but it had little to non-impact on the overall
poster design yet. This all changed around 1880 with the invention of
Jules Cheret 's so-called „3 stone lithographic process" - a breakthrough
which allowed lithographers to produce a wide spectrum of colours from just
three stones - usually using primary colours of red, yellow and blue. In
that sense, low-cost colour posters at last became a reality, and many artists
began to experiment, despite having strong and powerful backgrounds in fine
art or painting.

Cheret's process nevertheless still demanded superb artistry and craftsmanship.
The result was worthwhile - a remarkable intensity of colour and texture,
with sublime transparencies and nuances impossible in other media (even to this day).
The ability to combine word and image in such an attractive and economical format
finally allowed the lithographic poster to usher in the modern age of advertising.
If the poster was absent from the history, the probability of graphic design not
existing would be a reality. Nevertheless, it is important to acknowledge an
important figure in history, who has opened many doors in terms of better
printing for the young artists to cling to.

Known as the "father of the fine art poster", Jules Cheret not only developed
a cheaper colour lithographic process, with richer more expressive colours, but
he has also enhanced the aesthetic nature of the poster, endowed it with graceful
designs (some influenced by Ukiyo-e woodblock prints from Japan), and transformed
it into an independent work of art. Furthermore, he encouraged other painters
to explore the genre, because of the skill most painters had. He also introduced
the feminine form into his designs, for extra viewer-appeal. His female subjects
became so popular that Parisians dubbed them Cherettes. In total, Cheret produced
more than 1,000 posters, beginning with his 1867 advertisement for Sarah Bernardt's
performance as Princess Desiree in the comedy La Biche au Bois. Cheret's work
is celebrated throughout the world, and, by being honoured in 1928 with the opening
of the Cheret Museum in Nice, Jules Cheret's posters, are some of the most highly
sought-after items from the late 19th century.
